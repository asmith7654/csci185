Introduction to Series

What is a Series?

In math, a series is a concept that involves adding up an infinite number of terms. A series is usually denoted by the symbol Î£ (sigma) followed by an expression that defines the terms of the series.

There are many different types of series, and they can be classified based on their convergence or divergence properties. Some series converge to a finite limit, while others diverge to infinity or oscillate between positive and negative values. Series are an important tool in calculus and are used to represent functions, approximate values, and solve equations, among other things.

Infinite divisions of a square

An interesting application of series is the infinite division of a square. Imagine you have a 1 x 1 square. Divide that square in half. The area of one of those two sections is 1/2. Now divide one of those halves in half (the square now consists of a 1/2 area square, and two 1/4 area squares). The area still adds up to 1 for the total square. But what if we continue doing this infinite amount of times? If the process of splitting up the square never stops, does the area ever reach 1?

Before we answer the question of what the true area is, we must establish the process for evaluating series. We can evaluate a series by taking the nth partial sum, and evaluating as n approaches infinity. This process is very similar to what we did for improper integrals.

The nth partial sum, or sn, is the summation of a series, ending with the expression used to generate the terms to the nth value. N can equal any value, but to find the sum of a series, we will need n to approach infinity. Side note: Some questions may ask you to evaluate a partial sum to some constant (n = constant). All that this means is you must add n terms of the series together to get the desired result.

Now that we know what the nth partial sum is and how to evaluate series, lets apply our knowledge to the original question. Our nth partial sum for the infinite division of a 1 x 1 square looks something like 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + 1/16 ... + 1/2^n-1 + 1/2^n. The series ends with 1/2^n because each term is one higher degree of 2 as you progress, and we are taking the nth partial sum.

Through a bit of algebra, we can simplify this series. First, multiply both sides by 2. It should now look like 2 * sn = 2/2 + 2/4 + 2/8 + 2/16 ... 2/2^n. Notice that 2/2^n = 2/2^(n-1)*2 = 1/2^(n-1), and all of the terms beforehand simplify to 1 + 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 ... Now, we can rewrite our equation like so: 2 * sn = 1 + sn - 1/2^n. Subtract the sn from both sides and your new equation is sn = 1 - 1/2^n. To evaluate the series, we must take the limit as n approaches infinity of the sn. The limit as n approaches infinity of 1 - 1/2^n is 1 - 0 or 1. We have now proven that the summation of an infinite amount of divisions of a 1 x 1 square is equal to 1!
