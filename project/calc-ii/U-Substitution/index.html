U-Substitution

What is u-Substitution?

U-Substitution, also known as the "reverse chain rule," is a technique used in calculus to simplify integrals. It involves substituting a complex expression with a simpler one by using a new variable u.

The basic idea is to identify a part of the integrand that looks like the derivative of some simpler expression. We then substitute that expression for u, and rewrite the entire integral in terms of u.

How to use u-Substitution?

  1. Identify a part of the integrand that looks like the derivative of some simpler expression.
  2. Let u be that simpler expression.
  3. Calculate du/dx (the derivative of u with respect to x).
  4. Substitute u and du/dx into the integral.
  5. Simplify the integral by replacing all occurrences of x and dx with u and du, respectively.
  6. Integrate the new expression in terms of u.
  7. Finally, replace u with the original expression you substituted to get the final answer.

Examples of u-Substitution

Here are some examples of how to use u-Substitution:

Example 1:

Calculate the integral of x*cos(x^2) dx.

  1. Let u = x^2.
  2. Calculate du/dx = 2x.
  3. Substitute u and du/dx into the integral:

    4. Integral of cos(u) (1/2)du.

  4. Simplify the integral:

    5. (1/2)sin(u) + C, where C is the constant of integration.

  5. Replace u with the original expression:

    6. (1/2)sin(x^2) + C.
