Telescoping Series

What is a Telescoping Series?

A telescoping series is a series where many of the terms cancel out, leaving only a finite number of terms to be added. In other words, a telescoping series is a series where the partial sums simplify to a finite expression.

An example of a telescoping series would be Σ n=1 ∞ [1/n+1 - 1/n+2]. If you expand the terms out, it looks like: 1/2 - 1/3 + 1/3 - 1/4 + 1/4 - 1/5 ... 1/n-1 - 1/n + 1/n - 1/n+1 + 1/n+1 - 1/n+2. As you can see, all of terms cancel out except for 1/2 and -1/n+2. The partial sum would be 1/2 - 1/n+2.

You can then find the summation of the series by taking the limit of the nth partial sum as n approaches infinity. The limit above would become: 1/2 - 1/∞+2. 1/∞+2 will grow closer and closer to zero as n approaches infinity, so the sum of the series is 1/2.

How to find the sum of a telescoping series

  1. Look for something in the form of Σ [a/b - a/c]. Sometimes you will find a fraction that you can use partial fraction decomposition on, and after it has been decomposed it will follow the form mentioned at the beginning.
  2. Expand the terms and look for terms that cancel.
  3. Reduce to leftover terms. You now have the nth partial sum.
  4. Take the limit as n approaches infinity of the nth partial sum.
  5. If the answer is a finite value, the sum of the series converges to said finite value. If the answer is some form of infinity or something oscillating, the series diverges.
